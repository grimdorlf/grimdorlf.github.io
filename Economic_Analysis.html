Economic Analysis

Created Sunday 25 May 2014
(Based Mainly on Bejan,1996 cph. 7)

  • In a general way, an economic comprises various tasks as
    1. Estimating capital investment costs
    2. Calculating the main product costs under realistic assumptions for inflation, cost escalation, cost levelization, depreciation, taxes, and plant financing.
    3. Evaluating the profitability of alternative investments.
  • The cost is the amount of money paid to acquire or produce an item.
  • The market price is affected not only by the production cost and the desired profit of an item, also by other factors as demand, supply, competition, regulation and subsidies.
  • The total cost comprises the fixed and variable costs.
  • Cost estimation is useful in all the design stages for decision making. But it is common to make an economic analysis once the
flow sheet and equipment were specified.
  • There is an approach called revenue requirement method, which is formed by 4 main steps:
    1. Estimate the total capital investment.
    2. Determine the economic, financial, operating and market input parameters for the detailed cost calculation.
    3. Calculate the total revenue requirement.
    4. calculate the levelized product cost.

Total Capital Investments

  • An investment cost is an one-time cost.
  • Cost estimates for FCI (Fixed Capital Investment) comprises the direct and indirect costs.
  • Other outlays (apart from FCI) can be startup costs, working capital, licensing costs, research and development, and allowance for funds used during construction.

Cost Estimates for Purchased Equipment

  • Is the first step in a detailed cost estimation.
  • Includes spare parts and components.
  • The best estimates are through vendor's quotations.
  • Shipping and installation treated separately.
  • For large projects, quotations must be obtained at least for most expensive equipments.
  • The next best source are past purchase orders, quotations from cost estimators or calculations from proprietary databases.
  • Some software packages can assist the estimation, but may not necessarily be better than the data from charts.

Cost indices


  • For cost data based on conditions at different time, a Cost Index (CI) is used to adjust the cost info according to the year used as basis (BY) for the estimation, given a cost at different year (Y0).
  • Available CIs include:
    1. Chemical Engineering Cost Index. published in Chemical Engineering Magazine. Accessible through EBSCO (after SINAB Login - see Web Sources).
    2. Marshall and Swift Cost Index. published in the Oil and Gas Journal.
    3. Nelson-Farrar Refinery Cost Index.
    4. Engineering News Record (ENR). published in the Engineering News Record Magazine.
    5. U.S Boreau of Labor statistics. Published monthly in the Monthly Labor Review.

  • For individual items, Marshall is recommended, while the chemical engineering CIs are recommended for total plants or groups of components.
  • CIs applied to single components must be applied with caution when considering time intervals of 10 years or more.
  • CIs should not be applied to equipment to equipment for which the prices has been significantly affected by technology renewal or competition/regulation.
  • However for large groups of equipment, CIs provide a reasonable estimate of average cost escalation with time.

Definitions/Acronyms

  • CI: Cost index. Is an inflation indicator used to correct the costs of equipment, material, labor and supplies to the date of the estimate.
  • Direct costs: In a FCI are those costs of all permanent equipment, materials, labor and other resources involved in the fabrication, erection and installation of the permanent facilities.
  • FCI: Fixed Capital Investment, represents the total system costs including the design and construction period.
  • Fixed costs: Do not depend strongly on the production rate (depreciation, taxes on facilities, insurance, maintenance, rent, etc.).
  • Indirect costs: In a FCI are those costs that do not become a permanent part of facilities.
  • Off-site costs: Costs of the resources over the battery limits (imported or exported) as raw materials, utilities, distribution, roads, waste treatment, storage, offices, etc.
  • On-site costs: Costs of the installed equipment within the facilities, including supports and connections.
  • TCI: Total Capital Investment, corresponds to the FCI plus other outlays.
  • Variable costs: Depends directly on production rate (Raw materials, labor, fuel, etc.).
