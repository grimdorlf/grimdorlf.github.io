Exergy applications

Created Saturday 30 July 2016
@exergy

Use of exergy as optimization criteria

  • process elements showing the highest exergy losses are potential candidates for optimization.
  • typical solution strategy is restricted to favouring better technology where applicable.
  • when considering changes to specific elements, the structural interactions of the process should be included (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999), neglecting that can lead to a inferior solutions rather than improvements.
  • (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999) suggest that heuristics can be used to determine the reasons for exergy destruction.
  • (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999) mention that exergy functions don't show numerical advantages compared with conventional cost functions, and the importance of including economic constraints to avoid optimal solutions with large capital costs.

structural coefficients approach

  • method proposed by Beyer in 1971 apud (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999) that asses the effect of changes in the energy loss of an element (i.e. equipment, operation, etc.) with respect to a process process variable (i.e. temperature, pressure, etc.) on the total loss of the system. This method aims the optimization of a given process, considering possible changes in its structure (interaction among process elements).
  • implies excessive flowsheet calculations, because various sensivity studies become necessary (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
  • is suitable when key process variables were easily identified (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999). and when analysing the effects of specific modifications.

exergy in process synthesis

  • Traditional approaches to the synthesis and design of energy-intensive systems relied in the past on the intuition of experienced engineers and designers (El-Sayed, 2003).
  • Thermodynamic considerations are the underlying basis of most of the heuristics rules used in process synthesis (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
  • Trying to apply heuristics in a process optimization ( multicomponent separation sequence for instance) would not be helpful since they are frequently conflicting. However the use of heuristics can be used for reducing the solution space for subsequent optimization (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
  • In process syntesis, exergy criteria is well suited for pre-screening of alternatives. This can be done through extensive analysis of candidate structures (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
  • Evaluating the impact of known heuristic rules on the total exergy loss and the ratio of inner and outer losses for the respective subsystems leads to a set of heuristics that can be used to develop better superstructures (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
  • since optimality with respect to exergetic considerations does not necessarily lead to economically profitable process changes, cost functions will remain the better choice. (Rücker & Gruhn, 1999).
