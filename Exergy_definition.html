Exergy definition

Created Wednesday 30 November 2016
@exergy

Exergy is an extensive thermodynamic property of a system defined as the maximum work developed in the ideal process from a given state to the environmet state (Sciubba, 2005), or alternatively as the minimum theoretical useful work required to form a quantity of matter from substances present in the environment and to bring the matter to a specified state (Bejan, Tsatsaronis, & Moran, 1996) . It is an attribute fo the pair system-environment and it is associated to the quality of diferent energy forms understood as the measure of its capacity of cause change (Kotas, 1985). In that sense, 'high quality' forms of energy (e.g. potential, kinetic, mechanical, electrical) can be converted into each other with 100% efficiency, while Lower forms of energy (internal energy, chemical energy, thermal radiation, turbulent kinetic energy) cannot be converted into each other without an intrinsic efficiency loss. That loss are 'dissipated' into a low-temperature flux absorved or provided by the environment and is consequence of the irreversibility (entropy generation) inherent to real processes (Sciubba, 2005)(Bejan, 2006) . Consequently, exergy is not conserved:// in every real process, there is an exergy destruction caused by irreversibility effects, including the processes where all the initial capabilty to develop work is destroyed spontaneously. Since no work is needed to effect such a spontaneous change, it follows that the value of exergy is at least zero and therefore cannot be negative, comprising those systems receiving thermal energy from// environment (Bejan, Tsatsaronis, & Moran, 1996). However, (Sciubba & Wall, 2007) mentions thta there may be particular combinations of the values of the thermodynamic parameters of a system such that its exergy value is negative; in these cases, to bring the system in equilibrium with the reference environment, work must be done on the system by the environment.
The environment can be understood as a system so large that its properties are not altered by the interaction with the man-made system (Sciubba, 2005). It is normally regarded as composed of common substances existing in abundance within the Earth's atmosphere, ocean and crust, in such a way that there is no possibility of developing work from interactions between parts of itself.

The chemical exergy is the maximum theoretical useful work obtainable as the system passes from the thermomechanical equilibrium to a complete equilibrium with the environment (Bejan, Tsatsaronis, & Moran, 1996).

Chemical exergy

For the calculation of the chemical exergy of a system, the substances comprising the system must be referred to the properties of a suitable selected set of environmental substances. Given the difficulty and the inconvenience of modelling thoroughly the composition of the natural environment, the notion of the standard chemical exergy of a substance was introduced, which is related to it in a standard state (Tn=298.15, Pn=101.325 kPa), with the assumption that the conventional mean concentrations of a set of reference species contained in the reference environment (RE) have been taken into account. These species generally fall into three groups: gaseous components of the atmosphere, solid substances from the lithosphere, and ionic and nonionic substances from the oceans resembling as closely as possible the chemical makeup of natural environment. The reasoning of this approach and the detailed procedure of calculation of standard chemical exergy (boch) are presented in (Szargut, Morris, & Steward, 1988); the values for reference species, as well as for many common substances are tabulated in molar basis.
