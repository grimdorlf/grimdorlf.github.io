Absorption chillers

Created Saturday 02 January 2016
@CHP

Definition

Absorption chiller is a technology that allows cooling to be produced from heat, rather than from electricity.

Opportunities of use

It is unlikely that absorption cooling will replace conventional systems on a large-scale. But there are many applications where it can offer an environmentally and economically superior alternative. Absorption chillers is worth considering if one of the following factors apply:

  • If there is a CHP unit and cannot use all of the available heat, or you are considering a new CHP plant.
  • Waste heat is available.
  • A low cost source of fuel is available (e.g. landfill gas)
  • Your boiler efficiency is low due to a poor load factor (particularly in summer).
  • Your site has an electrical load limit that will be expensive to up-grade.
  • Your site is particularly sensitive to noise or vibration.
  • Your site needs more cooling but has an electric load limitation that is expensive to overcome, and you have an adequate supply of heat.

Environmental effects

Most potential applications occur in which absorption chillers will be less environmentally damaging than alternatives: generally when the source of heat would otherwise be wasted. The absence of CFCs and other potentially damaging refrigerants is not a true environmental advantage, because the environmental impact of the cooling plant is dominated by the energy consumption of the heat source (for absorption chillers) or the power station (for electrical chillers).

Benefits

the only moving parts are small internal pumps. Thus, Absorption units operate with much less noise and vibration than the conventional alternatives. In addition, these pumps consume much less power than the compressor of a mechanical unit. Where a limitation of power supply exists ans is expensive to overcome, the absorption chillers offers an alternative that can avoid upgrading the supply.

Availability

There are two main types of absorption chillers:

  • Lithium bromide / water systems: from 100 kW to thousands of kW of cooling capacity. The major limitation of this type of units is the chilled water temperature (generally above 5°C), because they use water as refrigerant and cannot be solved by adding an anti-freeze agent.
  • Ammonia / water systems: Standard units have sizes from 100 - 800 kW, although small (30-100 kW) direct-fired are available too. The refrigerant is the ammonia. Temperatures as low as -60°C are possible.

COP and performance

COP is the cooling effect produced divided by the energy input to the system and is dimensionless. The COP of an absorption unit is typically about 80% of the ideal COP.

Temperature range

The vast majority of units operate above 5°C (chilled water). Some special units can operate below 5°C (ETSU, 2001).

Type of heat source

Heat is normally delivered to the absorption unit as hot water, steam or by burning natural gas directly. Other sources, such as hot gases require special design (ETSU, 2001).

Number of effects

In double and triple (under development) effect units, some of the heat is recycled internally to improve efficiency, but these units require a higher temperature heat source. Double effect units work tipically with heat sources above 140°C. This is normally available from exhaust gases, from steam at 7-9 barg or pressurized hot water at 160°C.

Heat rejection

Absorption units reject more heat than the mechanical ones, this means that the cooling towers or dry air coolers will be larger (and expensive). At the temperatures that occur in conventional dry air coolers in the summer weather, the cooling output of an absorption chiller is dowgraded more dramatically than a conventional chiller.
